# docs/product-intent.md

## The User Problem

People save content constantly: articles, LinkedIn posts, YouTube videos, Reddit threads, tweets. They save with good intentions. They never return to it. The backlog grows. It becomes a pile to ignore. The habit of saving dies because there is no reward loop.

This is saved-content hoarding. The tool exists to solve it.

---

## The Product Promise

Save anything in under five seconds. Get a trustworthy summary and clear action pointer immediately. Return to your saves on a rhythm. Know what to do with each item. Archive or act. Move on.

---

## The Target Behavior Change

Before this tool: user saves 50 things, returns to zero of them, forgets the app exists.

After this tool: user saves regularly, returns every few days via digest, takes action on at least some items, and feels the loop is closed rather than open-ended.

Success is not a bigger database of saved links. Success is the user doing something because of what they saved.

---

## Success Criteria by Phase

### v0.1
- User saves at least 5 items in the first week without it feeling annoying.
- Summaries feel useful and trustworthy, not noisy or generic.
- Capture from Mac and iPhone both work reliably.

### v0.2
- User opens the digest email and returns to the app.
- User interacts with at least one item per digest cycle (archives it, acts on it, or marks it for later).

### v0.3
- User does not feel overwhelmed by backlog.
- The most important items are surfaced visibly.
- Low-value clutter is suppressed or grouped away.

### v1
- The app surfaces items the user actually engages with.
- The user feels the app understands their interests and work context.
- The experience feels like a thoughtful assistant, not a passive storage bin.

---

## What This Is Not

- Not a read-later app (Pocket, Instapaper). Those solve storage, not action.
- Not a note-taking app. Content is captured from external sources, not created.
- Not a social bookmarking tool. This is personal and private.
- Not a research aggregator. The goal is action, not collection.
